Best Renovation Contractor in Malaysia – Portfolio & Reviews

✍️ John Lim One Stop Renovation
📍 KL · Selangor · Seremban
⏱ 7 min read

Finding a reliable renovation contractor in Malaysia is one of the most stressful parts of any home project. Horror stories of incomplete work, vanishing contractors, blown budgets, and poor workmanship are unfortunately common — and often preventable with the right due diligence.

This guide gives you the real criteria for evaluating any renovation contractor in KL, Selangor, or Seremban — so you can make an informed decision before signing anything or paying a single ringgit.

1. Check for CIDB Certification First

The Construction Industry Development Board (CIDB) is Malaysia's official regulatory body for the construction and renovation industry. A CIDB-registered contractor has undergone screening, registered their workers, and is legally accountable under Malaysian construction law.

Why does this matter to you?

  • Some banks require CIDB-certified contractors for renovation loan disbursement
  • CIDB registration means the contractor is traceable and accountable — not a fly-by-night operation
  • It signals a baseline of professionalism and commitment to the industry

How to verify: Ask the contractor for their CIDB certificate number and verify it at cidb.gov.my. Any legitimate contractor will provide this without hesitation.

2. Review Their Portfolio — In Detail

Any contractor worth hiring will have a substantial portfolio of completed work. When reviewing a portfolio, look beyond the pretty photos:

  • Is the work consistent across different projects? One great project could be luck. Ten great projects is a track record.
  • Does the portfolio include projects similar to yours? If you're renovating a condo, look for condo projects. If it's a landed home, look for that.
  • Are there before-and-after shots? These show the actual transformation, not just a photogenic finished space.
  • Can you speak to past clients? A confident contractor will connect you with references. Reluctance is a red flag.

3. Insist on an Itemised Quotation

This is non-negotiable. Never accept a lump-sum quote like "full renovation for RM 80,000." You have no way to evaluate whether that's fair, what's included, or what's missing.

A proper itemised quotation should break down:

  • Every carpentry item (wardrobe, kitchen cabinet, TV console) with dimensions and material specified
  • Flooring area, tile grade, and cost per square foot
  • Electrical points, wiring scope, and M&E works line by line
  • Paint brand, number of coats, and areas covered
  • Demolition scope — what is hacked, what is protected
  • Timeline with milestones and payment schedule tied to progress
Red Flag

If a contractor quotes you a lump sum and refuses to break it down, walk away. Vague quotes are how unexpected "additional costs" appear mid-project — when you've already paid a deposit and have nowhere to go.

4. Understand Their Subcontractor Structure

Many renovation contractors in Malaysia are essentially project managers who outsource every single trade — carpentry to one company, tiling to another, electrical to a third. This creates a chain of subcontractors where:

  • Each link adds markup, inflating your costs
  • Coordination between trades is slower and error-prone
  • Accountability becomes murky when something goes wrong
  • Quality control suffers because the main contractor isn't present at every stage

In contrast, a contractor with in-house trades — their own carpentry team, their own tilers, their own painters — delivers faster, more consistent, and more cost-efficient work.

5. Verify Google Reviews — With Scepticism

Google reviews are useful but need careful reading. Here's how to assess them properly:

  • Volume matters: 50+ reviews is more meaningful than 5 reviews, even if those 5 are 5-star
  • Look at negative reviews: How does the contractor respond? A professional response shows accountability; an aggressive or dismissive one reveals character
  • Check review recency: A burst of 20 reviews in a single month followed by silence is suspicious
  • Read the content: Genuine reviews mention specific details (worker names, project types, timeline). Generic reviews ("great service!") add little signal

6. Clarify Warranty Terms in Writing

A workmanship warranty is your safety net if defects emerge after handover — hairline cracks, cabinet doors that won't close, tiles that lift, paint that peels. Every reputable contractor should offer one.

Key questions to ask:

  • What exactly is covered — workmanship only, or materials too?
  • How long is the warranty period?
  • What is the response time for warranty claims?
  • Is the warranty term included in the contract?

7. Red Flags That Should End the Conversation

  • ✕
    Requests a large upfront deposit (>30%) — Standard practice is 10–30% deposit, with the rest tied to milestones
  • ✕
    Cannot provide a written contract — Any legitimate contractor works with a signed agreement
  • ✕
    Offers a price far below market rate — Suspiciously low quotes usually mean corners will be cut on materials or labour
  • ✕
    Refuses to give references or show past work — A confident contractor has nothing to hide
  • ✕
    Vague timeline with no milestones — "We'll finish in about 3 months" is not a project timeline
  • ✕
    Poor communication before the project starts — If they're hard to reach now, imagine during construction
  • ✕
    No physical office or verifiable business address — Fly-by-night contractors are real, and they're exactly as described

8. The Right Questions to Ask Before Hiring

When you sit down with a potential contractor, bring this list:

  1. Are you CIDB certified? Can I see the certificate?
  2. Do you have in-house workers or do you subcontract all trades?
  3. Can you provide an itemised quotation with material specifications?
  4. What is your standard payment schedule?
  5. Do you offer a written workmanship warranty? For how long?
  6. What is your estimated timeline for my project, with milestones?
  7. Who will be my point of contact during the project?
  8. Can you connect me with two past clients I can speak to directly?
